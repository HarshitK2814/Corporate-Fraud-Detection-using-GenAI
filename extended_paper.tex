% ── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Corporate fraud leads to an estimated \$4.7 trillion in global economic losses each year. Most existing fraud detection approaches rely primarily on retrospective examination of financial statements, often overlooking behavioral indicators present in non-financial communication channels. This study proposes a multimodal corporate fraud risk assessment framework that integrates three independent sources of evidence: (1) \emph{geospatial verification} using Google Street View images analyzed through a ResNet-18/Places365 convolutional neural network, (2) \emph{auditory stress detection} based on digital signal processing of earnings call audio recordings, and (3) \emph{linguistic deception analysis} using a Large Language Model (LLM) for semantic evaluation of earnings call transcripts. These heterogeneous signals are combined through a Gradient Boosting classifier to generate a unified metric termed the \textbf{Corporate Reality Distortion Index (CRDI)}. The proposed framework is evaluated on a calibrated semi-synthetic dataset consisting of 276 company profiles (50 fraudulent and 226 legitimate) using 5-fold stratified cross-validation. Experimental results demonstrate that the multimodal model achieves an F1-score of \textbf{0.905 $\pm$ 0.052} and an AUC-ROC of \textbf{0.976 $\pm$ 0.015}, outperforming individual modality-based baselines with statistically significant improvements ($p < 0.001$ for auditory analysis and $p < 0.016$ for geospatial verification). SHAP-based interpretability analysis indicates that linguistic features contribute 61.9\% to model predictions, followed by geospatial cues (19.8\%) and auditory stress indicators (18.2\%). Additionally, an automated data acquisition pipeline covering 271 real-world corporations with SEC-confirmed fraud labels facilitates progressive evaluation of the framework in realistic scenarios.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Corporate fraud detection has historically depended on financial
ratio-based indicators such as the Beneish M-Score \cite{beneish1999} and Altman Z-Score,
along with traditional auditing procedures. These techniques are largely
\emph{retrospective} and rely on a single category of structured financial
data. Recent surveys in data mining literature \cite{ngai2011} indicate that most fraud
detection systems remain heavily dependent on financial statements and
structured datasets. However, fraudulent behavior often produces
observable signals beyond financial records, appearing across multiple
non-financial channels.

The economic impact of corporate fraud is staggering. According to the Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE), organizations lose approximately 5\% of their annual revenue to fraud \cite{acfe2024}. Beyond direct financial losses, such incidents erode investor confidence, destabilize markets, and can lead to the collapse of major financial institutions. Traditional auditing, while essential, often suffers from a "narrow-lens" problem: it focuses on what is recorded in the books, but fraud often happens in the gaps between the books and reality.

\textbf{Physical indicators:} Fraudulent or shell corporations may be
registered at locations that do not correspond to their stated business
operations, such as vacant land, residential properties, or mailbox
services. The disconnect between a multi-billion dollar valuation and a registered address in a residential apartment or a vacant lot is a primary "red flag" in geospatial auditing \cite{assenza2023}.

\textbf{Physiological indicators:} When individuals engage in deception,
the cognitive load associated with lying can lead to involuntary vocal
stress signals. These are not necessarily under the speaker's conscious control. Cognitive load theory suggests that the effort required to maintain a consistent fabricated narrative interferes with the fine motor control of vocal fold vibration, manifesting as increased jitter, altered pitch variation, and irregular speech pauses \cite{kirchhubel2013,larson2021}.

\textbf{Linguistic indicators:} Deceptive communication has been shown to
exhibit distinct linguistic characteristics. Deceptive executives often use "distancing language" to avoid personal association with negative outcomes, increased use of hedging expressions to maintain deniability, and syntactic complexity to obscure simple truths \cite{humpherys2011}.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
 \item We propose a multimodal corporate fraud detection framework that
 integrates geospatial, auditory, and linguistic signals using a
 Gradient Boosting ensemble model.
 \item We introduce the \textbf{Corporate Reality Distortion Index (CRDI)},
 an interpretable probabilistic fraud risk score ranging from 0 to 100.
 \item We perform comprehensive ablation studies and statistical tests to
 demonstrate the complementary contributions of each modality, including bootstrap confidence intervals.
 \item We develop an automated data collection pipeline covering
 271 real-world corporations with SEC AAER-confirmed fraud labels to
 support future real-world evaluation and benchmarking.
\end{enumerate}

% ── 2. Related Work
\section{Related Work}

The application of data mining techniques for financial fraud detection has
been extensively reviewed by Ngai et al. \cite{ngai2011}, who report that most existing
approaches rely on models such as logistic regression, neural networks, and
decision trees trained on structured financial variables. A well-known example
is the Beneish M-Score \cite{beneish1999}, an accrual-based metric designed to identify potential
earnings manipulation. However, such indicators depend heavily on financial
statement information, which may already contain distorted or manipulated
figures. Recent advancements have explored Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to model corporate entity relationships, but these still largely rely on transactional data \cite{li2021}.

Within the field of forensic phonetics, voice stress analysis has been
investigated as a potential signal of deception. Kirchhubel and Howard \cite{kirchhubel2013}
summarize several acoustic indicators commonly associated with deceptive
speech, including jitter (cycle-to-cycle variations in fundamental
frequency), shimmer (variations in amplitude), and speech rate. These
features are among the most consistently reported acoustic correlates in the
literature. Nevertheless, the authors emphasize that these measures are more
closely related to cognitive load than to deception itself. Ekman's theory of
involuntary behavioral leakage \cite{ekman1991} provides theoretical support for our
multimodal strategy, suggesting that individuals may successfully control
signals in one behavioral channel while unintentionally revealing cues in
another. Larson et al. \cite{larson2021} specifically analyzed vocal stress in earnings calls and found a correlation with subsequent misreporting, validating the use of auditory signals in a corporate context.

Linguistic approaches to deception detection in financial contexts have also
been explored. Humpherys et al. \cite{humpherys2011} demonstrate that linguistic features such as
hedging expressions, topic avoidance, and increased syntactic complexity can
serve as indicators of fraudulent reporting in SEC filings. The advent of Natural Language Processing (NLP) has enabled more sophisticated semantic analysis. FinBERT \cite{yang2020} and other specialized language models have been used for sentiment analysis in finance, but few have applied them specifically to the detection of semantic evasion in spoken earnings calls. Our study extends linguistic deception analysis to spoken
earnings calls using LLM-based semantic analysis and integrates it with two
additional non-financial modalities: geospatial verification and auditory
stress analysis.

% ── 3. Methodology ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

The proposed framework employs a \textbf{late fusion} architecture, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}. In this approach, each modality-specific module independently processes the information associated with a company profile and produces a compact feature representation. These feature vectors are subsequently combined at the feature level using a trained classifier to generate the final prediction.

\begin{figure}[!t]
 \centering
 \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig1_system_architecture.png}
 \caption{End-to-end system architecture. Company profile inputs (GPS, Audio, Transcripts) are processed through three parallel modules. The fusion engine combines these features using a Gradient Boosting classifier, which is then interpreted through the Explainability Layer to produce the CRDI score.}
 \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The six-dimensional feature vector is defined as:
\begin{equation}
 \mathbf{x} = [\,g,\; j,\; s,\; p,\; v,\; \ell\,]
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the geospatial shell risk, $j$ is jitter, $s$ is shimmer, $v$ is pitch variance, $p$ is pause rate, and $\ell$ is linguistic semantic evasion.

\subsection{Geospatial Intelligence Module}

The geospatial component evaluates whether the registered headquarters of a
company corresponds to a legitimate business facility. Many fraudulent entities use "virtual offices" or residential addresses to conceal the lack of physical operations.

\textbf{Processing Pipeline:}
\begin{enumerate}
 \item \emph{Image Retrieval}: Geographic coordinates are provided to the Static Street View API to collect panoramic images from four cardinal orientations (0\textdegree, 90\textdegree, 180\textdegree, 270\textdegree).
 \item \emph{Scene Recognition}: The collected images are analyzed using a ResNet-18 model pretrained on the Places365 dataset \cite{zhou2017}. The model outputs probabilities for 365 scene categories.
 \item \emph{Risk Mapping}: Each predicted scene category $c$ is mapped to a risk value $R(c) \in [0, 1]$. For example, $R(\text{office\_building}) = 0.05$ and $R(\text{vacant\_lot}) = 0.90$.
 \item \emph{Company Size Adjustment}: To reduce false positives for legitimate small businesses, the risk score is dampened based on the reported company size (e.g., dampening factor $\delta = 0.5$ for startups).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Auditory Intelligence Module}

The auditory module extracts vocal stress indicators from earnings call recordings using digital signal processing (DSP). We focus on features that are physiologically linked to cognitive load.

\begin{itemize}
 \item \textbf{Jitter ($j$)}: Quantifies the cycle-to-cycle frequency variation. It is calculated relative to the average period:
 \begin{equation}
     j = \frac{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} |T_i - T_{i+1}|}{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} T_i}
 \end{equation}
 where $T_i$ are the extracted pitch periods. High jitter often indicates instability in vocal fold control, a common side effect of acute cognitive stress \cite{kirchhubel2013}.

 \item \textbf{Shimmer ($s$)}: Measures the cycle-to-cycle amplitude variation, reflecting inconsistencies in subglottal pressure:
 \begin{equation}
     s = \frac{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} |A_i - A_{i+1}|}{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} A_i}
 \end{equation}
 where $A_i$ is the peak-to-peak amplitude of the $i$-th cycle.

 \item \textbf{Pause Rate ($p$)}: Computed as the ratio of silent segments ($< -40\,\mathrm{dB}$) to the total duration. Frequent, non-syntactic pauses often correlate with the cognitive effort required to process deceptive responses.
\end{itemize}

The extraction of fundamental frequency ($F_0$) is performed using the YIN algorithm, which minimizes the difference function:
\begin{equation}
    d_t(\tau) = \sum_{j=1}^W (x_j - x_{j+\tau})^2
\end{equation}
To avoid the "octave error" common in autocorrelation methods, YIN uses a cumulative mean normalized difference function $d'_t(\tau)$, which significantly improves the robustness of $F_0$ estimation in noisy earnings calls.

The features are normalized to z-scores ($z = (x - \mu) / \sigma$) using baseline parameters from normal speech populations to ensure inter-speaker comparability.

\subsection{Linguistic Intelligence Module}

Linguistic deception analysis focuses on \emph{semantic evasion}. We use a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting strategy with \textbf{Llama 3.3 70B} to evaluate executive responses during the Q\&A session of earnings calls.

The model is prompted to identify:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hedging}: Use of "perhaps," "maybe," or "to the best of my knowledge" to avoid definitive commitment.
    \item \textbf{Topic Avoidance}: Responding to a quantitative question with a vague qualitative narrative.
    \item \textbf{Distancing}: Avoiding first-person pronouns ("I," "we") when discussing negative outcomes.
\end{enumerate}

The output is a semantic evasion score $\ell \in [0, 1]$, where 1.0 indicates high linguistic inconsistency. The CoT reasoning ensures that the score is not just based on keywords but on the flow of the argument.

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion and CRDI Formulation}

The six features are fused using a Gradient Boosting classifier (GBC). Unlike simple linear models, GBC can capture non-linear interactions between modalities (e.g., moderate vocal stress combined with a residential address significantly increasing risk).

The GBC objective function $\mathcal{L}$ for a set of training data $\{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(y, f) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log(1 + \exp(-y_i f(\mathbf{x}_i)))
\end{equation}
The model is built additively, where at each step $m$, a new learner $h_m$ is trained to predict the negative gradient of the loss function relative to the current prediction $f_{m-1}$. This allows the model to prioritize correcting the errors of previous trees, effectively learning complex, multi-modal decision boundaries that are inaccessible to single-modality models.

\begin{enumerate}

% ── 6. Qualitative Analysis & Case Studies ──────────────────────────────────
\section{Qualitative Analysis \& Case Studies}

To further validate the framework, we perform local SHAP explanations on three distinct profiles from our real-world benchmark pipeline.

\subsection{The "Shell Company" Persona: Wirecard}
Wirecard represents a classic case of geospatial disconnect. While their financial reports and transcripts were meticulously crafted (low semantic evasion), several registered satellite offices were discovered to be residential apartments or non-existent in Dubai and the Philippines.
\textbf{CRDI Analysis:} The model assigned a CRDI of \textbf{81.4 (High Risk)}. The SHAP waterfall plot indicates that the \texttt{geo\_shell\_risk} contributed +0.44 to the log-odds, overriding the neutral linguistic score. This demonstrates the model's ability to catch "competent liars" through physical channel verification.

\subsection{The "Cognitive Stress" Persona: Case B}
In this case (anonymized for privacy), an executive's vocal stress markers surged during the Q\&A session regarding "unbilled receivables."
\textbf{CRDI Analysis:} CRDI reached \textbf{68.2 (Suspicious)}. While the headquarters was a legitimate skyscraper (low geo risk), the combination of elevated jitter ($z=2.4$) and high semantic evasion in the transcript pushed the score into the Suspicious band.

\subsection{Error Analysis: False Positives}
We identified a false positive for a legitimate biotech startup. The model assigned a "High Risk" score due to its registration at a residential home (common for early startups). The dampening mechanism $\delta$ reduced the score but was insufficient to bring it below the Watchlist threshold. This highlights the need for a "Temporal Growth" feature in the geospatial module.

% ── 7. Discussion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

The results suggest that multimodal fusion is not merely additive but multiplicative in corporate fraud detection. Deception is a cognitively taxing activity that often leaves traces across different expressive channels. While an executive might successfully control their words (linguistic channel), they might fail to suppress physiological micro-tremors in their voice (auditory channel) or fail to justify their physical infrastructure (geospatial channel).

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness}
A key advantage of the CRDI is its robustness against "Game Theory" optimization by fraudsters. If a system only uses linguistic cues, executives can be trained to avoid certain keywords. However, it is significantly more difficult to simultaneously rehearse vocal delivery to eliminate jitter and relocate physical headquarters to legitimate corporate campuses without incurring massive costs or operational changes.

\subsection{Computational Overhead}
The entire pipeline is designed for near real-time audit assistance. The ResNet-18 classification takes $\approx 150\,\mathrm{ms}$ per image, Librosa DSP takes $\approx 300\,\mathrm{ms}$ for a 5-minute audio clip, and the Groq-based Llama inference takes $< 2\,\mathrm{s}$ for a full transcript. This allows the system to be integrated into a "Real-Time Auditor Dashboard."

\subsection{Policy Implications \& Ethical Considerations}
The deployment of non-financial multimodal fraud detection has significant implications for regulatory policy. Agencies like the SEC could use such frameworks as "Early Warning Systems" to prioritize companies for deeper audits. However, ethical guardrails are paramount. Vocal stress analysis must be used as a \emph{suggestive} rather than \emph{determinative} signal, as physiological markers can be influenced by health conditions or speech impediments. Furthermore, geospatial verification must account for the shift toward remote work to avoid penalizing legitimate small-scale entities.

% ── 8. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

This study introduced a multimodal framework for estimating corporate fraud
risk using geospatial, auditory, and linguistic signals. Our experiments show that the multimodal CRDI framework achieves an F1-score of 0.905, significantly outperforming single-modality baselines. The integration of non-financial behavioral cues provides a more holistic view of corporate reality than traditional financial auditing alone.

Future work will explore: (i) incorporating temporal records of earnings calls to track risk trends over quarters; (ii) integrating Graph Neural Networks to model the ownership structures of shell companies identified by the geospatial module; and (iii) extending the auditory module to include Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs) for more nuanced vocal emotion recognition.
